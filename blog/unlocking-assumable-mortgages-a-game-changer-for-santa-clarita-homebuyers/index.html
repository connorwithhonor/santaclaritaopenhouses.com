What Are Assumable Mortgages?

An assumable mortgage is a home loan that a buyer can take over from the seller, inheriting the existing terms, including the interest rate, remaining balance, and repayment period. Instead of applying for a new mortgage at current market rates, the buyer “assumes” the seller’s loan, potentially locking in a lower rate from years past. This can be a game-changer in a high-interest-rate environment, like the one we’re seeing in 2025.

Why Are Assumable Mortgages Rare?

Assumable mortgages aren’t as common as they once were. Most conventional loans today are not assumable without lender approval, and even when allowed, the process can be complex. However, certain government-backed loans—such as VA, FHA, and USDA loans—often include assumable provisions, though they come with specific eligibility requirements. For example, VA loans may require the buyer to be a qualified veteran or meet credit standards, while FHA loans might need lender approval. In Santa Clarita, where homes range from affordable condos to luxury properties, these loans represent a small but valuable segment of the market. The Catch with Assumable Mortgages

While assumable mortgages sound like a dream, they come with challenges. The buyer must qualify for the loan assumption, which involves a credit check and financial review by the lender. Additionally, if the home’s value exceeds the remaining loan balance, the buyer needs to cover the difference, either with cash or a second loan. Low Interest Rates in a High-Rate Environment

Imagine this: a seller in Canyon Country has a VA loan from 2020 with a 2.5% interest rate. Current 30-year fixed rates are hovering around 6.5% in 2025. For a $600,000 home with a $400,000 assumable loan, the savings could be upwards of $500 per month compared to a new loan at 6.5%.
