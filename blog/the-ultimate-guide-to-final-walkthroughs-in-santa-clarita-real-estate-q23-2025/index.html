What Is a Final Walkthrough?

The final walkthrough is the buyer’s last opportunity to inspect a property before escrow closes, typically scheduled 24–48 hours before the closing date. It’s not a full home inspection—that happens earlier in the process—but a focused check to confirm the property is in the agreed-upon condition, all repairs are complete, and no new issues have surfaced. For sellers, it’s about proving you’ve met your contractual obligations, from leaving the home clean to ensuring utilities are on.

Real-World Final Walkthrough Scenarios in Santa Clarita

Let’s explore some common scenarios you might encounter during a final walkthrough in Santa Clarita, along with solutions:

Scenario 1: Uncompleted Repairs

Issue: The seller agreed to fix a broken garage door, but it’s still malfunctioning during the walkthrough.

Solution: Document the issue with photos and notify your realtor immediately. You can request a credit, delay closing until the repair is complete, or renegotiate terms. A written repair agreement is crucial here.

Scenario 2: Missing Fixtures

Issue: The seller removed a chandelier that was supposed to stay with the home.

Solution: Refer to the purchase agreement to confirm what was included in the sale. Your realtor can negotiate a replacement or credit. Learn more about purchase agreements at https://www.santaclaritaopenhouses.com/blog/the-2024-residential-purchase-agreement-for-a-home-buyer-buying-real-estate.

Scenario 3: Utilities Turned Off

Issue: The electricity is off, so you can’t test the appliances or HVAC system.

Solution: Contact the seller’s agent to have utilities turned back on. If this isn’t possible before closing, request a delay or a contingency to verify systems post-closing.

Scenario 4: Tenant Holdover

Issue: A tenant is still living in the property and refuses to leave, as seen in foreclosure scenarios.

Solution: Coordinate with the seller or bank to initiate eviction proceedings. Delay closing until the property is vacant. My YouTube Short (https://youtube.com/shorts/hRcTnL3vl0w?feature=share) highlights this issue.

Scenario 5: New Damage

Issue: You notice water damage from a recent storm that wasn’t present during the home inspection.

Solution: Document the damage and notify your realtor. You may need to file an insurance claim or renegotiate with the seller to cover repair costs.

Common Misconceptions About Final Walkthroughs

There are several myths about final walkthroughs that can trip up buyers and sellers. Let’s debunk a few:

Myth 1: The Walkthrough Is Just a Formality

Reality: The walkthrough is your last chance to catch issues before closing. Skipping it or rushing through can lead to costly oversights, like missing a broken HVAC system or uncompleted repairs.

Myth 2: All Repairs Are Optional

Reality: If repairs are agreed upon in writing during escrow, they’re binding. As I noted in my YouTube Short (https://youtube.com/shorts/hRcTnL3vl0w?feature=share), any issues not addressed should have been discussed and documented beforehand.

Myth 3: Buyers Can Demand New Repairs

Reality: The walkthrough isn’t for identifying new issues—it’s for verifying agreed-upon conditions. New issues may require negotiation or insurance claims.

Myth 4: Sellers Can Leave Personal Items

Reality: Unless specified in the contract, the home should be empty and clean. Leaving items behind can lead to disputes.

For more clarity on real estate myths, check out https://www.santaclaritaopenhouses.com/blog/santa-clarita-real-estate-faqs.

How to Handle Disputes During the Final Walkthrough

Disputes during the final walkthrough can derail a closing, but they don’t have to. Here’s how to handle common conflicts:

1. Uncompleted Repairs

If a repair wasn’t done, refer to the written agreement. Your realtor can negotiate a credit, delay closing, or arrange for the repair to be completed post-closing with an escrow holdback.

2. New Damage

If new damage appears (e.g., water damage from a storm), document it with photos and notify your realtor. You may need to file an insurance claim or renegotiate with the seller.

3. Missing Fixtures

If an item included in the sale is missing, check the purchase agreement. Your realtor can request a replacement or credit.

4. Tenant Holdovers

In foreclosure cases, tenants may refuse to leave. Your realtor should coordinate with the seller or bank to resolve the issue, potentially delaying closing.

5. Utilities Off

If utilities are off, request the seller to restore them. If that’s not possible, negotiate a contingency to test systems post-closing.
